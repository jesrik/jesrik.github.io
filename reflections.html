OpenAI Ethics Reflection

If I was a software engineer at OpenAI and had a say in releasing the project or research, I would choose not to. The reason why is simple--there are people out there who would use it to generate misinformation. For example, it could be used to spread misinformation on a platform such as Facebook.

There is no practical automated or manual way to distinguish OpenAI-generated content from “real” content. A huge number of issues would arise, such as how would a company go about protecting their platform from unidentifiable spam or fake content? Furthermore, if the project or research were publicly released, no one would have control over who gets to use it--if it is available for one person, it is available for everyone.

OpenAI chose to release partial research because they identified the huge problems the project and research would cause. We do not have the necessary knowledge or technology to handle the issues that would arise; therefore, the cons outweigh the pros. However, artificial intelligence is still in an infant state and society can gain tremendous benefits from it, which is why it is important to continue advancing it--this is the reason why I believe OpenAI still chose to release partial research despite the problems it could cause.

Cumulative Reflection

I can think of three main learning activities outside of the classroom that have helped me develop skills to acquire and apply new knowledge, these are: my co-op and internships, hackathons, and the career fair.
I have had two internships and one co-op. This is by far the best way to gain practical industry knowledge and experience, and also to develop specific areas of interest within the field. My first technical internship was at the Iowa Department of Transportation, where I was on the Highway Division and focused on an Electronic Permit System, specifically the Adopt-A-Highway program in Iowa. I learned that government agencies are not always up-to-date with technology stacks and a lot of the work involves maintaining legacy code. My next internship was at Western Digital, where I was a Data Engineer for the Data Analytic Engineering team. I got exposure to modern industry standards and technology stacks, and I also learned that I have great interest in continuous integration and continuous development. Lastly, I had a co-op at Western Digital, where I was a Hardware Development Engineer. I learned about an entirely new different field and fell in love with it. Prior to this experience, I had absolutely no idea about hardware design, verification, and manufacturing. I learned industry practices and found my passion, which is why I accepted a position with this team.
Hackathons are a great way to build projects for your resume and get internships. They pressure you into trying new technologies, and place a hard deadline.
The career fair is an excellent way to get practice for interviews, and also knowledge check you against the industry. It is a fantastic way to build confidence in yourself, and also set goals.
